What Canadians Get Wrong About Capital Gains Tax (2026 Update)

Rick Minji ‚Ä¢ February 13, 2026 ‚Ä¢ 11 min read

Capital gains tax is one of the most misunderstood areas of Canadian taxation. The 2024 changes to the inclusion rate made things even more confusing, and misinformation is everywhere ‚Äî from dinner party conversations to social media "tax tips" that are flat-out wrong.

This guide cuts through the noise. We'll explain exactly how capital gains are taxed in Canada after the 2024 changes, bust the most common myths, and show you practical strategies to legally minimize what you owe. Use our Capital Gains Calculator to run your specific numbers.

üéØ Key Takeaway

  • Capital gains are not fully taxed ‚Äî only a portion (the "inclusion rate") is added to your income
  • Individuals: 50% inclusion on the first $250,000 of gains per year, 66.67% above that
  • Corporations and trusts: 66.67% inclusion on all capital gains (no $250K threshold)
  • Your principal residence is still completely exempt from capital gains tax
  • Small business owners get a $1.25 million Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE)
  • Strategic use of TFSAs, RRSPs, tax-loss harvesting, and timing can significantly reduce your bill

How Capital Gains Tax Works in Canada

When you sell an asset for more than you paid for it, the profit is called a capital gain. This applies to stocks, bonds, ETFs, investment properties, cottages, cryptocurrency, and most other non-registered investments.

Here's the critical thing most people get wrong: you don't pay tax on the entire gain. Only a portion ‚Äî called the inclusion rate ‚Äî gets added to your regular income and taxed at your marginal rate.

üìåThe Basic Formula

Taxable Capital Gain = Capital Gain √ó Inclusion Rate

If you buy a stock for $10,000 and sell it for $60,000, your capital gain is $50,000. At a 50% inclusion rate, only $25,000 is added to your income. If your marginal tax rate is 43%, you pay $10,750 in tax ‚Äî not $21,500 (which is what you'd owe if the full $50,000 were taxed).

What Triggers a Capital Gain?

  • Selling investments (stocks, ETFs, bonds, mutual funds) in a non-registered account
  • Selling real estate that isn't your principal residence (investment property, cottage, vacant land)
  • Selling cryptocurrency (yes, CRA treats crypto as a commodity)
  • Deemed dispositions at death (your estate is treated as if you sold everything at fair market value)
  • Gifting assets to someone other than your spouse (you're deemed to have sold at FMV)

What Doesn't Trigger a Capital Gain?

  • Investments inside a TFSA (completely tax-free, forever)
  • Investments inside an RRSP/RRIF (tax-deferred ‚Äî you pay income tax on withdrawal, not capital gains tax)
  • Selling your principal residence (exempt, but you must designate it)
  • Transferring to a spouse (rollover at adjusted cost base)

The 2024 Inclusion Rate Change

Before June 25, 2024, the inclusion rate was simple: 50% for everyone. Half your gain was taxable, period.

The 2024 federal budget changed this with a two-tier system effective June 25, 2024:

For Individuals

  • First $250,000 of capital gains per year: 50% inclusion (unchanged)
  • Above $250,000 per year: 66.67% inclusion (two-thirds)

For Corporations and Trusts

  • All capital gains: 66.67% inclusion (no $250,000 threshold)
‚ö†Ô∏èImportant: The $250K Threshold Resets Annually

The $250,000 threshold is per year, not per transaction and not lifetime. If you realize $200,000 in gains this year and $200,000 next year, both years are entirely at the 50% rate. But $400,000 in a single year means $250,000 at 50% and $150,000 at 66.67%. Timing your dispositions across tax years can save you thousands.

Common Misconceptions

Myth #1: "I pay 50% (or 67%) tax on my capital gains"

Wrong. The 50% (or 66.67%) is the inclusion rate, not the tax rate. The included portion is then taxed at your marginal income tax rate. If your marginal rate is 43% and you have a $100,000 gain:

  • Taxable amount = $100,000 √ó 50% = $50,000
  • Tax owed = $50,000 √ó 43% = $21,500 (effective rate of 21.5% on the gain)

Myth #2: "The new rules mean I pay 67% tax on big gains"

Also wrong. Even on gains above $250,000, the effective tax rate maxes out at about 36% (66.67% inclusion √ó ~54% top marginal rate). And that's only the portion above the $250K threshold.

Myth #3: "I need to report capital gains from my TFSA"

Nope. Gains inside a TFSA are completely tax-free. You never report them. This is one of the most powerful features of the TFSA ‚Äî unlimited, tax-free growth forever.

Myth #4: "If I don't sell, I don't owe anything"

Mostly true, but... Capital gains are only triggered on disposition (selling or deemed selling). However, at death, CRA treats you as having sold everything at fair market value (deemed disposition). Your estate will owe capital gains tax on any unrealized gains ‚Äî unless the assets transfer to a surviving spouse.

Myth #5: "I can give my cottage to my kids tax-free"

Wrong. Transferring property to anyone other than a spouse triggers a deemed disposition at fair market value. If your cottage has appreciated significantly, this can result in a massive tax bill. The only exception is if the cottage qualifies as your principal residence for those years.

Worked Examples

Let's run the actual math at different gain levels. We'll assume an Ontario resident with $100,000 of employment income (marginal rate ~43.41%).

Example 1: $50,000 Capital Gain

  • Entire gain is under $250K threshold
  • Taxable amount: $50,000 √ó 50% = $25,000
  • Approximate tax: $25,000 √ó 43.41% = $10,853
  • Effective tax rate on the gain: 21.7%

Example 2: $200,000 Capital Gain

  • Still entirely under $250K threshold
  • Taxable amount: $200,000 √ó 50% = $100,000
  • Approximate tax: ~$45,000 (pushes into higher brackets)
  • Effective tax rate on the gain: ~22.5%

Example 3: $500,000 Capital Gain

  • First $250,000 at 50% = $125,000 taxable
  • Next $250,000 at 66.67% = $166,675 taxable
  • Total taxable: $291,675
  • Approximate tax: ~$142,000
  • Effective tax rate on the gain: ~28.4%

Example 4: $1,000,000 Capital Gain

  • First $250,000 at 50% = $125,000 taxable
  • Next $750,000 at 66.67% = $500,025 taxable
  • Total taxable: $625,025
  • Approximate tax: ~$310,000
  • Effective tax rate on the gain: ~31%

The Principal Residence Exemption

Your principal residence is completely exempt from capital gains tax. This is one of the most valuable tax benefits in Canada ‚Äî and it's worth understanding the rules to protect it.

Qualifying Rules

  • You (or your spouse/kids) must have ordinarily inhabited the property in each year you designate it
  • A family unit can only designate one property per year as principal residence
  • You must report the sale on your tax return (Schedule 3) even though it's exempt ‚Äî failure to report can void the exemption
  • The land must be under 0.5 hectares (1.24 acres) unless you can show the excess was necessary for use and enjoyment
‚ö†Ô∏èDon't Forget to Report the Sale

Since 2016, CRA requires you to report the sale of your principal residence on your tax return, even if the gain is fully exempt. If you fail to report, CRA can deny the exemption and apply a late-filing penalty of $100/month up to $8,000. Always report the disposition on Schedule 3.

The Cottage Dilemma

If you own both a home and a cottage, only one can be your principal residence for any given year. The optimal designation strategy depends on which property has appreciated more per year. The formula:

Exempt gain = Total gain √ó (1 + years designated) √∑ years owned

The "+1" bonus year means you can cover up to two properties in the year of acquisition or sale. This is worth professional advice for families with significant property gains.

The Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE)

If you sell qualified small business corporation shares or qualified farm/fishing property, you may be eligible for the LCGE ‚Äî one of the most powerful tax shelters available to Canadian entrepreneurs.

2026 LCGE Limits

  • Qualified small business shares: $1,250,000 lifetime exemption (increased from $1,016,836 in 2024)
  • Farm and fishing property: $1,250,000 lifetime exemption
  • These amounts are indexed to inflation going forward

Qualifying Conditions

For small business shares to qualify:

  • At the time of sale, 90%+ of assets must be used in an active business carried on primarily in Canada
  • Throughout the 24 months before sale, more than 50% of assets must be active business assets
  • The shares must not have been owned by anyone other than you or a related person in the 24 months before sale
üí°Multiply the Exemption

Each individual gets their own LCGE. A married couple selling a qualifying small business can shelter up to $2.5 million combined. Some business owners also restructure to include adult children as shareholders before a sale, potentially sheltering $3.75M+ across three family members. This requires careful planning and professional advice.

Strategies to Minimize Capital Gains Tax

1. Use Your TFSA and RRSP

The simplest strategy: hold investments that are likely to generate capital gains inside tax-sheltered accounts. Gains inside a TFSA are never taxed. Gains inside an RRSP are tax-deferred (you pay income tax on withdrawal, but no capital gains tax specifically).

2. Tax-Loss Harvesting

Sell investments that are down in value to realize capital losses, which offset your capital gains dollar-for-dollar. Capital losses can be carried back 3 years or forward indefinitely.

‚ö†Ô∏èWatch the Superficial Loss Rule

If you sell a security at a loss and buy the same (or identical) security within 30 days before or after the sale, CRA will deny the loss. This also applies if your spouse, RRSP, or TFSA buys it. Wait at least 31 days, or buy a similar (but not identical) investment.

3. Timing Your Dispositions

Since the $250,000 annual threshold resets each year, spreading large gains across multiple tax years keeps more of your gains at the 50% inclusion rate. If you're selling a property or large stock position, consider whether you can close in January vs December to split gains across two tax years.

4. Spousal Transfers and Income Splitting

While you can't simply transfer investments to a lower-income spouse (attribution rules apply), there are legitimate strategies:

  • Prescribed rate loan: Lend money to your spouse at the CRA prescribed rate, they invest, and gains are taxed in their hands
  • Each spouse uses their own TFSA ‚Äî contribute to your lower-income spouse's TFSA with no attribution
  • Second-generation income: If you gift funds and they generate income, the income from that income (second generation) is taxed in the recipient's hands

5. Donate Publicly Traded Securities

If you donate publicly traded securities directly to a registered charity, the capital gain is completely exempt ‚Äî and you still get the full donation tax credit. This is far more tax-efficient than selling, paying tax, and donating the cash.

6. Consider Incorporation for Large Portfolios

For very large investment portfolios ($1M+), holding investments inside a corporation can provide some benefits ‚Äî but also significant complexity. Corporations don't get the $250K threshold, and the refundable tax mechanism is complex. Professional advice is essential here.

Reporting Capital Gains on Your Tax Return

Capital gains are reported on Schedule 3 ‚Äî Capital Gains (or Losses) of your T1 return.

What You Need

  • Adjusted Cost Base (ACB): What you paid, including commissions and fees
  • Proceeds of Disposition: What you received on sale, minus selling costs
  • T5008 slips: Your brokerage sends these for securities transactions (but they often don't include ACB ‚Äî you need to track this yourself)
  • Records for real estate: Purchase price, legal fees, renovation costs (capital improvements), selling costs
üí°Track Your ACB Religiously

CRA does not track your adjusted cost base for you. If you make multiple purchases of the same stock over time, you need to calculate the average cost per share. Tools like AdjustedCostBase.ca can help. Poor ACB tracking is one of the most common ‚Äî and expensive ‚Äî tax filing mistakes.

The Bottom Line

Capital gains tax in Canada is more favourable than most people think. Even after the 2024 inclusion rate increase, the effective tax rate on capital gains is roughly 21-31% for most Canadians ‚Äî well below their marginal income tax rate.

The key takeaways:

  • Use tax-sheltered accounts first. Max your TFSA and RRSP before investing in taxable accounts.
  • Track your ACB. This is your responsibility, and getting it wrong is costly.
  • Time your dispositions. The $250K annual threshold rewards spreading gains across years.
  • Report your principal residence sale. The exemption is valuable ‚Äî don't lose it by failing to report.
  • Harvest losses. Don't let losing investments just sit there when they can offset gains.
  • Get advice for large transactions. Selling a business, investment property, or cottage? A tax professional can save you multiples of their fee.

üéØ Quick Reference

  • Individuals: 50% inclusion up to $250K/year, 66.67% above
  • Corporations/Trusts: 66.67% on all gains
  • Principal residence: Fully exempt (must report sale)
  • LCGE: $1.25M lifetime for qualifying small business shares
  • TFSA gains: Tax-free, always
  • Loss carryover: Back 3 years or forward indefinitely
